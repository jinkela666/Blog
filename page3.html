E-waste pollution


The toxic e-waste around our ordinary life, Dec 11, 2019



"The amount of e-waste produced, such as smartphones is huge, but the recycling rate is low."

About 41.8 million tonnes of global e-waste was generated in 2014, of which 3 million tonnes came from small electronic products. Only a small percentage of such a large amount of e-waste is recycled from regular sources. Gadgets such as smartphones are often harder to get into formal recycling channels than other bulky electronics. When they are recycled, smartphones are smashed and smelted with other metal waste, making them a material of low recycling value.

"Electronic waste cross-border transactions are often prohibited, the illegal channels have a huge hidden danger."

The Basel convention prohibits illegal cross-border transshipment of e-waste, but illegal international trade in e-waste is still common. E-waste is illegally traded and eventually dismantled in countries and regions such as Nigeria, Ghana, India and China, posing a potential threat to the environment and the health of workers.

"Smartphones are made from a variety of materials, which contain toxic and harmful substances."

There are also many toxic and harmful substances hidden in smartphone components, such as lead, cadmium, chromium, mercury, arsenic, beryllium, antimony, nickel, PVC, brominated flame retardants and other heavy metals and toxic and harmful substances. If handled improperly, toxic and harmful substances may be released to the environment, causing environmental pollution and threatening human health.

"Toxic and harmful substances are used in the production process."

The bigger environmental and human health problem with smartphones is the use of toxic and harmful substances in their production. Cases of the use of hazardous chemicals have been reported; Some of the substances used in the production of smartphones can cause cancer, while others can cause various adverse effects on human health. Benzene and n-hexane are used to clean electronic displays, according to a 2014 report by Green America and others; Research by South Korea's sharp found that flammable solvents and regenerated toxic chemicals such as ethylene glycol ethers are also used in the production of semiconductors or display screens.

 

The solutions

"Recycle products and reuse existing resources to the maximum extent. "

For smartphones that eventually need to be scrapped, we need to establish a closed-loop recycling system. Consumers should be aware of e-waste recycling and hand over their smartphones that need to be scrapped to a formal recycling channel. Mobile phone brands should establish a sound recycling cycle system, recycle products through the reverse supply chain channel, assume the extension of producer responsibility, and cooperate with legal recycling and dismantling enterprises to ensure the formal dismantling of products and the regeneration of resources. Mobile phone brands should also actively promote the use of renewable resources and reduce the exploitation of new resources through the transmission of the supply chain. Finally, a closed-loop recycling system is formed for the renewable resources.

"Extend the service life of the product. "

The idea needs to be from the perspective of whole life cycle of products, from product design stage, all the way to the product of waste retrieving and dismantling process, considering the product maintenance, components can be reused, waste materials recovery, raw material and manufacturing process without the use of toxic and harmful substances, such as lower energy consumption to redesign products. A good product design can not only prolong the product life, benefit the environment and health, but also improve the economy of recycling renewable resources.

"What can we do as a consumer?"

Here are a few questions to ask yourself as you consider replacing your smartphone:
The phone still works (or even if the phone has some minor problems, the main function is still normal and can continue to be used without repair or simple repair) :

- Send to relatives and friends, continue to extend the service life of the product. Remember to clean your phone of personal information and data.

- Try to exchange products you no longer need for something useful to you through a formal exchange platform.

- If you just want to get rid of your phone: contact the official channel or its authorized third-party company to see if there is a trade-in service.

Your phone really needs to be scrapped, or if you still have a bunch of phones sleeping in a drawer:

- Try to ask if the brand or the third party designated by the brand authorization can provide product recovery service. It is your responsibility and obligation to return the product to the producer for recycling and disposal. It is also an important step for the producer to fulfill the extension of producer responsibility.
